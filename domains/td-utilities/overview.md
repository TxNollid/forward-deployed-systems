# T&D Utilities — Overview

## Industry Landscape

Transmission & Distribution (T&D) is the backbone of the electric utility industry. It covers everything between power generation and the customer's meter — high-voltage transmission lines, substations, distribution feeders, transformers, and the last-mile infrastructure that delivers electricity.

The U.S. T&D market alone represents ~$100B+ in annual capital investment. Globally, aging infrastructure, renewable integration, and electrification are driving unprecedented investment cycles.

## Key Player Types

- **Investor-Owned Utilities (IOUs):** Largest segment. Publicly traded, regulated by state PUCs. Examples: Duke Energy, Southern Company, NextEra, AEP, Dominion. Typically 1M+ customers.
- **Municipal Utilities (Munis):** City-owned. Smaller, less regulated, more agile but resource-constrained. ~2,000 in the U.S.
- **Electric Cooperatives (Co-ops):** Member-owned, rural-focused. ~900 in the U.S. Governed by elected boards.
- **Regional Transmission Organizations (RTOs/ISOs):** Manage the bulk power grid. PJM, ERCOT, MISO, CAISO, etc. Not asset owners — they're market operators.
- **Engineering/Construction Firms:** Build and maintain T&D infrastructure. Quanta, MYR Group, Pike Electric.

## Industry Trends

- **Grid Modernization:** Replacing 50-70 year old infrastructure with smart grid technology. SCADA upgrades, automated switches, distributed sensors.
- **Renewable Integration:** Solar, wind, and battery storage are fundamentally changing power flow patterns. The grid was designed for one-way flow; now it's bidirectional.
- **Electrification:** EVs, heat pumps, and data centers are driving load growth after decades of flat demand. Distribution planning models are being rewritten.
- **Wildfire Mitigation:** Post-PG&E, utilities in fire-prone areas are investing heavily in detection, vegetation management, and public safety power shutoffs (PSPS).
- **Workforce Crisis:** 25-30% of the utility workforce is retirement-eligible within 5 years. Institutional knowledge is walking out the door.
- **Regulatory Pressure:** PUCs demanding better reliability, faster storm restoration, and cleaner energy — while scrutinizing rate increases.

## Common Pain Points

- **Asset data is fragmented.** GIS says one thing, the asset management system says another, and the field crew's tribal knowledge is different from both.
- **Vegetation management is reactive.** Spending $6-10B/year industry-wide, mostly on cycle-based trimming instead of risk-based prioritization.
- **Outage response relies on human coordination.** Storm restoration involves hundreds of crews, mutual aid, logistics — mostly managed with spreadsheets and phone calls.
- **Capital planning is slow.** Prioritizing billions in infrastructure investment across thousands of assets with incomplete condition data.
- **Regulatory filings are manual.** Reliability reports (SAIDI, SAIFI, CAIDI) require aggregating data across multiple systems.
- **Inspection data is underutilized.** Drone imagery, LiDAR, field photos pile up faster than humans can review them.

## Regulatory Environment

Heavily regulated at state (PUC/PSC) and federal (FERC, NERC) levels. Every significant capital expenditure, rate change, and reliability standard is subject to regulatory approval. AI deployments must be explainable to regulators and must not compromise grid reliability or cybersecurity (NERC CIP). See regulations.md for detail.
